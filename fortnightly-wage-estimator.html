Good to know

Why the tax changes every fortnight

Payroll works out tax as if every fortnight of the year looked like this one. It multiplies this fortnight's gross by 26, finds the yearly tax on that figure, then takes 1/26th of it.

So a fortnight with several weekend or night shifts looks like a high-earning year, and a bigger slice is withheld. A quiet fortnight looks like a lower-earning year, so less is withheld. Because tax brackets are stepped, the take-home doesn't move in a straight line with the hours.

It evens out at tax time — over- or under-withholding is squared up in her annual return. For budgeting, the per-week figure above is the safe number to plan around.

The penalty rates used here

Saturday — 1.5× (time and a half). Sunday — 1.75× (time and three-quarters, not double; double-time only applies as a Sunday overtime rate). Weekday night — +20% loading. Public holiday — ~2.5× (an approximation of the public-health "1.5 days' pay extra" entitlement; the exact value depends on roster status).

Weekend rates replace the night loading rather than stacking on top of it. If she's casual, ordinary hours carry a 25% loading and weekend maths differs — set the base rate to her casual hourly rate to get close.

How accurate is this?

It's a close estimate, not a payslip. Real payroll uses the ATO's rounded withholding tables and may differ by a few dollars. It assumes she's an Australian resident claiming the tax-free threshold, and it doesn't include salary packaging, allowances, or other deductions. Treat it as a planning guide.
